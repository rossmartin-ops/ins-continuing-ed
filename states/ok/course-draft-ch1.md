# OK.INS-CE-LEG — Oklahoma Legislative Updates
# Chapter 1: Producer Conduct — New Standards and Enforcement

---

## Introduction

The 2023–2026 biennium brought significant changes to how Oklahoma holds insurance producers accountable for their conduct. Three major developments stand out: a new legal standard for annuity recommendations that requires producers to act in their clients' best interest, renewed enforcement attention to anti-rebating violations, and new regulatory guidance on how carriers must govern artificial intelligence systems — all of which have direct implications for producers in the field.

This chapter examines each of these changes, what they require of you, and how to stay in compliance.

---

## 1.1 The Annuity Best Interest Standard

### Background

For decades, Oklahoma insurance producers recommending annuities were held to a **suitability standard** — the requirement that a recommended product be suitable for the customer based on their financial situation and needs. While suitability provided a baseline of protection, critics argued it left room for producers to recommend products that were merely adequate rather than genuinely in the customer's best interest, particularly when higher-commission products were available.

In 2020, the National Association of Insurance Commissioners (NAIC) adopted a revised Suitability in Annuity Transactions Model Regulation (#275) designed to elevate the standard. The new model aligned with the Securities and Exchange Commission's Regulation Best Interest, which had applied to broker-dealers since 2020.

Oklahoma adopted NAIC Model #275 effective **September 1, 2023**, codified at Oklahoma Administrative Code (OAC) 365:25-17. With this adoption, Oklahoma became the **40th state** to implement the enhanced standard.

Oklahoma Insurance Commissioner Glen Mulready stated at the time: "The new rules will provide stronger protections for retirees and safeguard access for lower and middle-income families."

---

### Which Transactions Are Covered

The best interest standard applies to any **recommendation** regarding an annuity transaction, including:

- Purchasing a new annuity
- Exchanging one annuity for another (1035 exchange)
- Replacing an existing annuity
- Surrendering or withdrawing funds from an annuity

The word "recommendation" is key. The rule applies any time a producer's communication reasonably could be interpreted as a suggestion that the consumer take a particular action regarding an annuity. It does not require a formal sales presentation — a conversation at a client meeting in which a producer suggests that an annuity might be a good fit for a portion of the client's retirement savings is a recommendation under this standard.

The rule applies to **all annuity products** with a life contingency component. This includes fixed annuities, fixed indexed annuities, variable annuities, and immediate annuities. It does not apply to variable annuities that are securities products subject only to SEC oversight — those are covered by the SEC's Regulation Best Interest — but producers selling variable annuities in Oklahoma who are also licensed as insurance producers must be aware that they may face obligations under both frameworks.

---

### The New Standard

Under OAC 365:25-17, a producer making a recommendation regarding an annuity transaction must "act in the **best interest** of the consumer under the circumstances known at the time the recommendation is made."

Critically, a producer cannot "place the producer's or insurer's financial interest ahead of the consumer's interest." This is a meaningful departure from the prior suitability standard, which was silent on the question of whose interest took priority when producer compensation and client outcomes diverged.

---

### The Four Obligations

The best interest standard is organized around four specific producer obligations: **Care, Disclosure, Conflict of Interest, and Documentation**. Each obligation applies at different points in the recommendation process.

---

#### Care

The Care obligation requires that before making a recommendation, the producer:

- Knows the consumer's financial situation, insurance needs, and financial objectives
- Understands the available recommendation options given the consumer's profile
- Has a reasonable basis to believe the recommended annuity and transaction type are in the consumer's best interest

Consumer profile information includes: age, annual income, financial situation and needs, financial experience, financial objectives, intended use of the annuity, financial time horizon, existing assets (including investment and insurance holdings), liquidity needs, liquid net worth, risk tolerance, and tax status.

Gathering this information is not optional. A producer who skips the profile-gathering step and recommends a product without a documented basis for that recommendation does not satisfy the Care obligation — even if the recommendation turns out to be a good one.

The Care obligation is also **continuous**. It applies every time a producer makes a recommendation, not just at the point of initial sale. If a client's circumstances change significantly — a divorce, a job loss, a serious health event — and the producer makes a new recommendation without updating the profile, the obligation may not be met.

**What this means in practice:** If a consumer has limited liquid assets and a short time horizon, recommending a long-surrender-period annuity product with high surrender charges would likely fail the Care obligation even if the product is otherwise suitable in other respects. The producer must affirmatively consider whether the product fits the consumer's actual circumstances, not just whether the consumer can technically purchase it.

**When the consumer declines to provide information:** Occasionally a consumer will decline to answer questions about their financial situation — citing privacy concerns, distrust, or simply impatience. If a consumer provides incomplete information or refuses to answer key profile questions, the producer should document this refusal. In these circumstances, the producer can still make a recommendation, but must base it on the information that was provided and must note in the file that the consumer declined to provide complete profile information. The producer is not required to refuse to transact business simply because a consumer is uncooperative — but the lack of complete information should be reflected honestly in the documentation.

---

#### Disclosure

The Disclosure obligation requires that **prior to or at the time of a recommendation**, the producer discloses:

1. A description of the producer's relationship with the insurer (captive agent vs. independent agent)
2. The types of compensation received (commission, fees, trails, bonuses, or any other form)
3. Whether the producer receives more or less compensation depending on which product is recommended
4. Whether the producer has a material conflict of interest related to the recommendation

The disclosure must be made in a way that the consumer can understand it. Technical or legalistic language that obscures material conflicts does not satisfy the obligation.

**Timing matters:** The disclosure must happen before or at the point of recommendation — not after the consumer has already signed an application. A disclosure buried in post-sale paperwork does not satisfy this requirement.

The Oklahoma Insurance Department has made disclosure forms available on the OID website (oid.ok.gov). Producers should use current, OID-approved forms and retain signed copies.

---

#### Conflict of Interest

The Conflict of Interest obligation requires that a producer:

- Identifies any material conflict of interest related to a recommendation
- Discloses the conflict to the consumer (as required by the Disclosure obligation)
- Manages the conflict in a way that does not affect the recommendation to the consumer's detriment

A material conflict of interest exists when a producer has a financial interest in a transaction that could influence a recommendation. Common examples include:

- Recommending an annuity from a carrier that pays a higher commission than a comparable product from another carrier
- Receiving a bonus, incentive, or sales contest reward tied to volume from a specific carrier
- Having a proprietary or equity relationship with a specific insurer or product company
- Receiving non-cash compensation (trips, gifts, entertainment) from a carrier in connection with reaching sales thresholds

**Managing — not eliminating — conflicts:** The rule does not prohibit all conflicts. It requires that they be identified, disclosed, and managed. In most cases, proper disclosure to the consumer is sufficient management. A producer who earns a higher commission on Product A than Product B can still recommend Product A if it is genuinely in the consumer's best interest — provided the commission difference is disclosed and the recommendation is supportable based on the consumer's profile.

However, if a conflict is so pervasive that it cannot reasonably be managed without affecting the recommendation, the producer should not make the recommendation. For example, if a carrier is paying aggressive bonuses tied entirely to sales volume — and the bonus is substantial enough to create a strong financial incentive regardless of product suitability — the producer needs to examine honestly whether that incentive structure can be managed through disclosure alone.

---

#### Documentation

The Documentation obligation requires that a producer maintain records sufficient to demonstrate compliance with the best interest standard. At minimum, records should include:

- The consumer profile information gathered before making the recommendation
- The basis for the recommendation, including why the recommended product was in the consumer's best interest
- Any conflicts of interest identified, disclosed, and how they were managed
- Copies of all disclosures provided to the consumer
- Consumer acknowledgment of disclosures
- If the consumer declined to provide complete profile information, a notation to that effect

Records must be retained for at least **five years** following the date of the transaction or, if no transaction occurs, the date the recommendation was made.

**Why documentation matters:** The Documentation obligation is both a compliance requirement and a practical protection. If a consumer later disputes whether a recommendation was in their best interest, thorough contemporaneous records are the primary evidence of compliance. Producers who cannot produce documentation are in a seriously difficult position if an OID investigation or consumer complaint arises — and the passage of time will not help, because the standard requires contemporaneous records, not reconstructed ones.

**A practical documentation framework:** When working with annuity clients, consider maintaining a client file that includes the following at the time of each recommendation:

1. **Consumer Profile Form** — completed, signed, and dated by the consumer at the time of the meeting. Use an OID-approved form or your carrier's current form.
2. **Product Comparison Notes** — a brief written note documenting which products were considered, and why the recommended product was preferred. This does not need to be elaborate — a few sentences explaining the basis for the recommendation is sufficient.
3. **Disclosure Form** — signed acknowledgment of the producer's compensation arrangement and any conflicts.
4. **Recommendation Summary** — a short written statement connecting the consumer's profile (their age, time horizon, liquidity needs, etc.) to the features of the recommended product. Again, this does not need to be long — but it needs to exist.

The goal is not to create paperwork for its own sake. It is to create a contemporaneous record that would allow a neutral reviewer — an OID examiner, a compliance officer, or a judge — to understand what the producer knew about the client, what the producer considered, and why the recommendation was made.

**One additional note on replacement documentation:** When a recommendation involves replacing an existing annuity, the Documentation obligation becomes especially important. The producer should record: the current surrender value of the existing contract, any surrender charges that will be incurred, the projected benefits of the new product compared to the existing one, and the specific client profile factors that justify the replacement despite those costs. Without this analysis in writing, a replacement transaction is particularly difficult to defend.

**Electronic records are acceptable.** Producers who maintain records in a CRM, practice management system, or digital file are meeting the retention requirement as long as the records are complete, timestamped, and retrievable. OID has not required paper records. What matters is that records exist, are accurate, and can be produced if requested.

### Practical Scenarios

**Scenario 1 — The competing products situation:**

A 68-year-old retired schoolteacher contacts her producer about moving $150,000 from a bank certificate of deposit into a fixed annuity. The producer represents two carriers. Carrier A offers a 5% first-year commission on a seven-year surrender-period product; Carrier B offers an 8% first-year commission on a ten-year surrender-period product with comparable rates. The consumer mentions that she may need access to some of the funds in five years for a planned home renovation.

Under the best interest standard, the producer must recommend the product that best meets the consumer's needs given her stated liquidity concern. A seven-year surrender period is already problematic given the client's five-year horizon — a ten-year product is clearly worse. Recommending the higher-commission Carrier B product without adequate justification would violate both the Care and Conflict of Interest obligations. The producer must disclose the compensation difference, recommend the product that better fits the client's liquidity needs, and document the analysis. If neither product is well-suited given the consumer's five-year horizon, the producer should say so.

**Scenario 2 — The documentation gap:**

A 55-year-old client is planning for retirement in ten years. His producer recommends a deferred fixed indexed annuity with a ten-year accumulation period, which matches the client's timeline well. The producer genuinely believes this is the right product for the client's situation — and it probably is. But the producer does not complete a written profile form, does not document the product comparison, and does not retain a copy of the disclosure form the client signed, assuming the client's signature on the application is sufficient.

Two years later, the client files a complaint with OID alleging the recommendation was not in his best interest. Without contemporaneous documentation, the producer cannot demonstrate that the client's profile was gathered, that the recommendation was analyzed against alternatives, or that conflicts were properly disclosed. The complaint may result in an investigation, enforcement action, or license suspension — not because the recommendation was actually wrong, but because it cannot be shown to have been right. The Documentation obligation exists to prevent exactly this outcome.

**Scenario 3 — The replacement:**

A client holds an existing fixed annuity with three years remaining on its surrender period. A producer recommends replacing it with a new product from a different carrier — one that happens to pay a 7% first-year commission. The new product has a fresh ten-year surrender period.

Replacements require heightened scrutiny under the best interest standard. The producer must affirmatively analyze: What is the consumer giving up by surrendering the existing product? What are the surrender charges on the existing contract? Does the new product provide materially better terms that justify those charges? Is the new product's longer surrender period consistent with the consumer's financial time horizon and liquidity needs?

If the primary driver of the replacement is the 7% first-year commission — and the consumer is not materially better served by the new product — the recommendation fails the Care and Conflict of Interest obligations. The Documentation requirement is especially important in replacement situations: the producer should document specifically why the new product is superior to the existing one for this particular consumer.

### Training Requirements

The adoption of Model #275 also introduced new training requirements for producers with a life line of authority.

**New sellers (no prior annuity sales before September 1, 2023):** Producers who wish to begin selling annuities must complete a **4-hour approved annuity training course** before doing so. Producers who held a life line of authority but had not sold annuities prior to September 1, 2023 had until February 29, 2024 to complete the course.

**Existing sellers who completed prior OID-approved training before November 1, 2023:** Must complete either a new 4-credit-hour course **or** a new 1-credit-hour update course by April 30, 2024. The 1-hour update covers the specific changes introduced by Model #275 for producers already familiar with the prior suitability framework.

**New license holders (on or after September 1, 2023):** Cannot sell any annuity product until the 4-hour course is completed.

The required training covers: annuity types and contract features, taxation, suitable uses for annuity products, the appropriate standard of conduct under Model #275, ethical sales practices, replacement considerations, and disclosure requirements.

## 1.2 Anti-Rebating: OID Enforcement Focus

### The Law

Oklahoma's anti-rebating statute, **36 O.S. § 1204(8)(a)**, prohibits any insurer or producer from offering, paying, or giving any rebate of premiums payable on a contract, any special favor or advantage in dividends or other benefits, or any valuable consideration or inducement whatever not specified in the contract, to any person insured or applying for insurance.

This prohibition has been part of Oklahoma insurance law for decades. However, **OID Bulletin No. 2025-01**, issued January 17, 2025, signals that the Department is actively investigating and finding violations — making this a live enforcement priority, not a dormant rule.

### What OID Found

OID found evidence that producers and insurers were **negotiating returns of commissions** to secure or retain business accounts. In practice, this means a producer agrees to kick back a portion of their earned commission to an insured — or to reduce their commission in exchange for the insurer offering a lower effective premium — as an inducement to write or keep the account.

These arrangements may appear informal or even well-intentioned. A producer who feels competitive pressure might voluntarily offer to share their commission with a client as a goodwill gesture. A carrier might offer a "special arrangement" for a large account. Neither arrangement is lawful. OID Bulletin 2025-01 makes clear that they violate § 1204(8)(a) regardless of how they are structured or what motivated them.

### Why Anti-Rebating Laws Exist

Anti-rebating statutes serve an important consumer protection function that producers sometimes overlook: they ensure **uniform pricing**. When insurance rates are filed and approved by OID, they are approved on the assumption that every customer in the same risk class pays the same rate. Selective rebating means that customers who know to negotiate — typically large commercial accounts or financially sophisticated buyers — receive a different effective price than customers who do not know to ask.

From a regulatory standpoint, a rebated commission is functionally equivalent to an unapproved premium reduction. It undermines the rate-making process and disadvantages less sophisticated consumers who are already paying more for the same coverage.

### Prohibited Arrangements

Bulletin 2025-01 identifies the following as prohibited:

- Returning commissions, or any portion of commissions, as an inducement to write or retain business
- Offering reduced premiums in exchange for returned commissions
- Any arrangement that benefits the insured through commission returns, whether negotiated directly with the insured or arranged through the insurer

The bulletin is clear that intent does not matter. A producer who reduces their commission out of genuine competitive spirit is still violating the law if the result is a benefit to the insured that is not specified in the policy contract.

### Permitted Exceptions

**36 O.S. § 1204(8)(b)** preserves certain lawful arrangements, including differences in premium based on lawful risk classifications (such as age or health status), and dividends or retrospective premium arrangements that are part of the filed contract terms.

The key distinction is whether a benefit is **specified in the contract and consistent with filed rates**, versus whether it is an off-contract arrangement negotiated outside the standard policy terms. When in doubt, consult legal counsel before entering into any compensation-sharing arrangement with a client.

### A Common Misconception

Some producers believe that voluntarily reducing their own commission — rather than asking the insurer to reduce the premium — is lawful because the insurer is not involved. This is incorrect. Section 1204(8)(a) prohibits offering "any valuable consideration or inducement whatever not specified in the contract." A commission reduction that effectively passes value to the insured is still a rebate, even if the reduction comes solely from the producer's side and the insurer's books are unaffected.

The statute's plain language makes no exception for producer-only arrangements.

### Enforcement Consequences

Producers found in violation of the anti-rebating statute may face the same enforcement actions available to OID for any producer misconduct under 36 O.S. § 1435.13: probation, censure, license suspension, license revocation, or civil penalties. The seriousness of the consequences reflects the seriousness with which OID views uniform pricing as a foundational consumer protection.

### Scenario — The "Competitive" Arrangement

A commercial lines producer has a long-standing relationship with a local manufacturing company. The company's workers' compensation renewal is coming up, and a competing producer is offering to reduce their commission to undercut the renewal premium. Rather than lose the account, the producer agrees to return 2% of their commission to the insured as a credit applied to the renewal.

This arrangement violates 36 O.S. § 1204(8)(a). The producer is offering valuable consideration not specified in the contract as an inducement to keep the business. The fact that the motivation was competitive — and that the producer felt they had no choice — is not a defense. If an OID examination or complaint investigation surfaces the arrangement, the producer faces potential disciplinary action. The correct response to competitive commission pressure is to compete on service, expertise, and market access — not through off-contract inducements.

## 1.3 Carrier Commission Practices: Unfair Trade Practices

### The Issue

**OID Bulletin No. 2025-12** addresses a related but distinct compensation problem: carriers that change commission structures mid-year, or eliminate commissions entirely, affecting producers who were already appointed and actively selling those products.

OID identified carriers engaging in the following conduct:

- Removing applications from their websites to restrict consumer access to certain products
- Informally discouraging producers from selling their products
- Making mid-year changes to commission structures without advance notice
- Eliminating commissions on products where producers were previously appointed, commissions had historically been paid, and the carrier's own rate development had included producer compensation assumptions — all without providing producers any advance notice

### Why This Violates Oklahoma Law

Under **36 O.S. § 1209(A)**, Oklahoma prohibits unfair methods of competition in the business of insurance. OID's position in Bulletin 2025-12 is that unilaterally eliminating commissions under these circumstances — particularly when rate development assumed compensation costs — may constitute an unfair method of competition.

The Department stated: "Discontinuing commissions on any insurance products **disincentivizes producers from marketing these products** to those who need them." The concern is not limited to producer income. When carriers manipulate compensation structures to pull products from the market or reduce their availability, consumers lose access to coverage. That is the harm the unfair trade practices law is designed to address.

### What Producers Should Do

If you are appointed by a carrier that eliminates commissions mid-year or restricts product access without advance notice:

1. **Document everything.** Keep records of your appointment terms, prior commission schedules, and any carrier communications about changes.
2. **Report to OID.** Bulletin 2025-12 signals active monitoring. The OID contact for complaints is the Legal Division at oid.ok.gov.
3. **Consult your professional association.** Organizations like the Oklahoma Association of Health Underwriters or Independent Insurance Agents of Oklahoma may have resources or collective advocacy channels.
4. **Review your appointment agreements.** Some appointment contracts include terms governing compensation changes. Understanding your contractual rights is important before taking any action.

## 1.4 Artificial Intelligence in Insurance: What Producers Need to Know

### The Regulatory Development

**OID Bulletin No. 2024-11**, issued November 14, 2024, requires all authorized insurers in Oklahoma to develop and maintain a written **AI Systems (AIS) Program**. This bulletin is directed at carriers, not individual producers — but it has practical implications for producers operating in a marketplace where AI is increasingly used in underwriting, rating, and claims decisions.

### What Carriers Are Required to Do

The AIS Program required of carriers must address four areas:

**Governance:** Senior management must be accountable for AI outcomes. Carriers must have policies covering the full AI lifecycle — from model development through deployment and retirement — along with oversight committees and model management procedures.

**Risk Management:** Carriers must conduct data quality and bias analysis, validate models and test their performance, protect consumer privacy, and monitor for model drift — the tendency of AI models to perform differently over time as real-world conditions change from the conditions on which they were trained.

**Third-Party Management:** When carriers use AI tools built by vendors, they must conduct due diligence on those vendors, build audit rights into contracts, and ensure vendors cooperate with regulatory oversight.

**Compliance:** AI-supported decisions must comply with Oklahoma's Unfair Trade Practices Act (36 O.S. § 1204), the Unfair Claims Settlement Practices Act (36 O.S. § 1250.5), and applicable rating laws.

### The Core Principle: Carriers Own Their AI Outcomes

The most important takeaway from Bulletin 2024-11 is simple: **carriers — not their AI vendors — are responsible for the outcomes of AI-driven decisions.** A carrier cannot point to its algorithm vendor when an underwriting decision violates Oklahoma law. The carrier is the licensed entity; the carrier is accountable.

This principle aligns with NAIC's 2020 AI Principles: fairness, accountability, transparency, and system security.

### Why This Matters to Producers

**You may be the first person to notice a problem.** Producers are often closer to individual clients and their specific circumstances than a carrier's underwriting department. If a client receives an adverse rating, a coverage denial, or an unusual claims outcome that seems inconsistent with their actual situation, that disconnect may reflect an AI model that is not performing as intended — or one that has drifted from its original parameters.

**Discrimination via algorithm is still discrimination.** Under the Unfair Trade Practices Act, unfair discrimination in underwriting or rating is prohibited regardless of whether it originates from a human decision-maker or an automated system. A carrier that uses AI to generate discriminatory outcomes cannot use the algorithm as a shield against liability.

**Producers have a reporting option.** If you believe a carrier's AI-driven decision has adversely and unfairly affected a client, you can report this concern to OID. The Department has authority — under Bulletin 2024-11 — to request AI program documentation, model inventories, validation records, and third-party vendor contracts during examinations.

**AI in your own practice.** Producers increasingly use AI tools for their own work: client communication drafts, lead generation, document preparation, market analysis. These tools can be genuinely useful. But producers should be clear-eyed about one thing: **using an AI tool does not shift your responsibility.** If an AI drafts a client communication that contains inaccurate information about a policy, you are responsible for that inaccuracy. If an AI suggests a course of action for a client and you follow it without adequate analysis, you are responsible for the outcome. AI is a tool, not a substitute for professional judgment.

## Chapter 1 Key Takeaways

**1. Annuity Best Interest Standard (OAC 365:25-17, effective September 1, 2023)**
Oklahoma adopted NAIC Model #275. Producers must now act in the consumer's best interest when recommending any annuity transaction — not merely recommend suitable products. Four obligations apply: Care (gather profile, analyze options), Disclosure (before recommendation, disclose compensation and conflicts), Conflict of Interest (identify, disclose, and manage), and Documentation (maintain contemporaneous records for at least five years). Training requirements apply to new and existing annuity sellers. Replacements require heightened analysis.

**2. Anti-Rebating (OID Bulletin 2025-01, 36 O.S. § 1204(8)(a))**
OID is actively investigating commission kickback arrangements. Returning any portion of a commission — or arranging a premium reduction in exchange for a commission return — as an inducement to write or keep business violates Oklahoma law. The prohibition exists to protect consumers through uniform pricing. Intent is not a defense.

**3. Carrier Commission Practices (OID Bulletin 2025-12, 36 O.S. § 1209(A))**
Carriers that unilaterally eliminate commissions mid-year or restrict product access without advance notice may be committing unfair methods of competition. OID has signaled active enforcement. Affected producers should document the situation and report to OID.

**4. Artificial Intelligence (OID Bulletin 2024-11)**
Carriers must maintain a written AI Systems Program. Carriers — not AI vendors — own the outcomes of AI-driven decisions. Discriminatory AI outcomes remain illegal. Producers play a meaningful role in identifying and reporting AI-driven problems. Using AI tools in your own practice does not reduce your professional responsibility for the advice you provide.
