## Introduction

The 2023–2026 biennium brought significant changes to how Oklahoma holds insurance producers accountable for their conduct. Three major developments stand out: a new legal standard for annuity recommendations that requires producers to act in their clients' best interest, renewed enforcement attention to anti-rebating violations, and new guidance on how carriers must govern artificial intelligence systems — all of which have direct implications for producers in the field.

This chapter examines each of these changes, what they require of you, and how to stay in compliance.

---

## 1.1 The Annuity Best Interest Standard

### Background

For decades, Oklahoma insurance producers recommending annuities were held to a **suitability standard** — the requirement that a recommended product be suitable for the customer based on their financial situation and needs. While suitability provided a baseline of protection, critics argued it left room for producers to recommend products that were merely adequate rather than genuinely in the customer's best interest, particularly when higher-commission products were available.

In 2020, the National Association of Insurance Commissioners (NAIC) adopted a revised Suitability in Annuity Transactions Model Regulation (#275) designed to elevate the standard. The new model aligned with the Securities and Exchange Commission's Regulation Best Interest, which had applied to broker-dealers since 2020.

Oklahoma adopted NAIC Model #275 effective **September 1, 2023**, codified at Oklahoma Administrative Code (OAC) 365:25-17. With this adoption, Oklahoma became the **40th state** to implement the enhanced standard.

Oklahoma Insurance Commissioner Glen Mulready stated at the time: "The new rules will provide stronger protections for retirees and safeguard access for lower and middle-income families."

### The New Standard

Under OAC 365:25-17, a producer making a recommendation regarding an annuity transaction must "act in the **best interest** of the consumer under the circumstances known at the time the recommendation is made."

Critically, a producer cannot "place the producer's or insurer's financial interest ahead of the consumer's interest." This is a meaningful departure from the prior suitability standard, which was silent on the question of whose interest took priority when producer compensation and client outcomes diverged.

### The Four Obligations

The best interest standard is organized around four specific producer obligations: **Care, Disclosure, Conflict of Interest, and Documentation**. Each obligation applies at different points in the recommendation process.

---

#### Care

The Care obligation requires that before making a recommendation, the producer:

- Knows the consumer's financial situation, insurance needs, and financial objectives
- Understands the available recommendation options
- Has a reasonable basis to believe the recommended annuity and transaction type are in the consumer's best interest based on the consumer's consumer profile information

Consumer profile information includes factors such as age, annual income, financial situation and needs, financial experience, financial objectives, intended use of the annuity, financial time horizon, existing assets (including investment and insurance holdings), liquidity needs, liquid net worth, risk tolerance, and tax status.

The Care obligation is continuous — it applies every time a producer makes a recommendation, not just at the point of initial sale.

**What this means in practice:** If a consumer has limited liquid assets and a short time horizon, recommending a long-surrender-period annuity product with high surrender charges would likely fail the Care obligation even if the product is otherwise suitable. The producer must affirmatively consider whether the product fits the consumer's actual circumstances, not just whether the consumer can technically purchase it.

---

#### Disclosure

The Disclosure obligation requires that **prior to or at the time of a recommendation**, the producer discloses:

1. A description of the producer's relationship with the insurer (captive vs. independent)
2. The types of compensation received (commission, fees, trails, bonuses, or any other form)
3. Whether the producer receives more or less compensation depending on which product is recommended
4. Whether the producer has a material conflict of interest related to the recommendation

The disclosure must be made in a way that the consumer can understand it. Technical or legalistic language that obscures material conflicts does not satisfy the obligation.

**Timing matters:** The disclosure must happen before or at the point of recommendation — not after the consumer has already signed an application. A disclosure buried in post-sale paperwork does not satisfy this requirement.

The Oklahoma Insurance Department has made disclosure forms available on the OID website. Producers should use current forms and retain copies.

---

#### Conflict of Interest

The Conflict of Interest obligation requires that a producer:

- Identifies any material conflict of interest related to a recommendation
- Discloses the conflict (as required by the Disclosure obligation above)
- Manages the conflict in a way that does not affect the recommendation to the consumer's detriment

A material conflict of interest exists when a producer has a financial interest in a transaction that could influence a recommendation. Common examples include:

- Recommending an annuity from a carrier that pays a higher commission than a comparable product from another carrier
- Receiving a bonus, incentive, or contest reward tied to sales volume from a specific carrier
- Having a proprietary relationship with a specific insurer

Managing a conflict of interest does not necessarily mean refusing to make a recommendation. In many cases, proper disclosure is sufficient. However, if the conflict is so significant that it cannot be managed consistent with the consumer's best interest, the producer must not make the recommendation.

---

#### Documentation

The Documentation obligation requires that a producer maintain records sufficient to demonstrate compliance with the best interest standard. At minimum, records should include:

- The consumer profile information gathered before making the recommendation
- The basis for the recommendation, including why the recommended product was in the consumer's best interest
- Any conflicts of interest disclosed and how they were managed
- Copies of disclosures provided to the consumer
- Any consumer acknowledgment of disclosures

Records must be retained for at least **five years** following the date of the transaction or, if no transaction occurs, the date the recommendation was made.

**Why documentation matters:** The Documentation obligation is both a compliance requirement and a practical protection. If a consumer later disputes whether a recommendation was in their best interest, thorough records documenting the producer's analysis and the consumer's profile information are the primary evidence of compliance. Producers who cannot produce contemporaneous documentation are in a difficult position if an OID investigation or consumer complaint arises.

---

### Training Requirements

The adoption of Model #275 also introduced new training requirements for producers with a life line of authority.

**New sellers (no prior annuity sales before September 1, 2023):** Producers who wish to begin selling annuities must complete a **4-hour approved annuity training course** before doing so. Producers who held a life line of authority but had not sold annuities prior to September 1, 2023 had until February 29, 2024 to complete the course.

**Existing sellers who completed prior OID-approved training before November 1, 2023:** Must complete either a new 4-credit-hour course **or** a new 1-credit-hour update course by April 30, 2024. The 1-hour update covers the specific changes introduced by Model #275 for producers already familiar with the prior suitability framework.

**New license holders (on or after September 1, 2023):** Cannot sell any annuity product until the 4-hour course is completed.

The 4-hour training content must cover: annuity types and contract features, taxation, suitable uses, the appropriate standard of conduct under Model #275, sales practices, replacement considerations, and disclosure requirements.

### Common Scenarios

**Scenario 1 — The competing products situation:**
A 68-year-old retired schoolteacher approaches her producer about moving $150,000 from a bank CD into a fixed annuity. The producer represents two carriers. Carrier A offers a 5% commission; Carrier B offers an 8% commission on a similar product with a longer surrender period. The consumer has mentioned she may need some of the funds in five years for a planned home renovation.

Under the best interest standard, the producer must recommend the product that best meets the consumer's needs given her stated liquidity concern. Recommending the higher-commission Carrier B product because of the producer's financial interest — without adequate justification based on the consumer's profile — would violate both the Care and Conflict of Interest obligations. The producer must disclose the compensation difference and document why the recommended product serves the consumer's best interest.

**Scenario 2 — The documentation gap:**
A producer makes what she believes is a genuinely good recommendation for a deferred annuity for a 55-year-old client planning for retirement in ten years. The recommendation appears well-suited. However, the producer does not document the consumer's profile information or the basis for the recommendation, believing the recommendation speaks for itself.

Two years later, the consumer files a complaint with OID alleging the recommendation was not in her best interest. Without contemporaneous documentation, the producer cannot demonstrate that the consumer's profile was gathered, that the recommendation was analyzed against available options, or that conflicts were disclosed. The Documentation obligation exists precisely for situations like this.

## 1.2 Anti-Rebating: OID Enforcement Focus

### The Law

Oklahoma's anti-rebating statute, **36 O.S. § 1204(8)(a)**, prohibits any insurer or producer from offering, paying, or giving any rebate of premiums payable on a contract, any special favor or advantage in dividends or other benefits, or any valuable consideration or inducement whatever not specified in the contract, to any person insured or applying for insurance.

This prohibition has been part of Oklahoma insurance law for decades. However, **OID Bulletin No. 2025-01**, issued January 17, 2025, signals that the Department is actively investigating and finding violations — making this a live enforcement priority, not a dormant rule.

### What OID Found

OID found evidence that producers and insurers were **negotiating returns of commissions** to secure or retain business accounts. In practice, this means a producer agrees to kick back a portion of their earned commission to an insured (or to reduce their commission in exchange for the insurer offering a lower premium) as an incentive to write or keep the account.

These arrangements may appear informal or incidental — a producer voluntarily reducing their commission, a carrier offering a "special arrangement" for a large account — but OID Bulletin 2025-01 makes clear that they violate § 1204(8)(a) regardless of how they are structured.

### Why Anti-Rebating Laws Exist

Anti-rebating statutes serve an important consumer protection function that producers sometimes overlook: they ensure **uniform pricing**. When insurance rates are filed and approved by OID, they are approved on the assumption that every customer in the same risk class pays the same rate. Selective rebating means that customers who know to negotiate — often large commercial accounts or sophisticated buyers — get a different effective price than customers who do not.

From a regulatory perspective, a rebated commission is functionally equivalent to a premium reduction that was never filed or approved. It undermines the rate-making process and puts less sophisticated consumers at a disadvantage.

### Prohibited Arrangements

Bulletin 2025-01 specifically addresses the following as prohibited:

- Returning commissions, or portions of commissions, as an inducement to write or keep business
- Offering reduced premiums in exchange for returned commissions
- Any arrangement that benefits the insured through commission returns, whether negotiated directly with the insured or facilitated through the insurer

The bulletin makes clear that the intent behind the arrangement does not matter. A producer who genuinely believes they are being generous or competitive by reducing their commission is still violating the law if the effect is an undisclosed benefit to the insured not specified in the policy contract.

### Permitted Exceptions

**36 O.S. § 1204(8)(b)** preserves certain lawful arrangements. Producers should review these exceptions carefully and consult legal counsel if uncertain whether a proposed arrangement falls within them. Examples of permitted arrangements include differences in premium rates based on lawful classifications such as age or health status, or payment of dividends and retrospective premium arrangements that are part of the filed contract.

The key distinction is whether the benefit is **specified in the contract and consistent with filed rates**, versus whether it is an off-contract arrangement negotiated outside the standard policy terms.

## 1.3 Carrier Commission Practices: Unfair Trade Practices

### The Issue

**OID Bulletin No. 2025-12** addresses a different but related compensation issue: what happens when **carriers** change commission structures — or eliminate commissions entirely — in ways that affect producers who were already appointed and selling the carrier's products.

OID found evidence of carriers engaging in the following conduct:

- Removing applications from their websites to restrict consumer access to certain products
- Informally suggesting that producers avoid selling their products
- Making mid-year changes to previously established commission structures without advance notice
- **Eliminating commissions on products** where independent agents were previously appointed, commissions were historically paid, and rate development had included compensation assumptions — all without advance notice to producers

### The Legal Concern

Under **36 O.S. § 1209(A)**, the Oklahoma Insurance Code prohibits unfair methods of competition in the business of insurance. OID's position in Bulletin 2025-12 is that unilaterally eliminating commissions under these circumstances may constitute an unfair method of competition.

OID stated directly: "Discontinuing commissions on any insurance products **disincentivizes producers from marketing these products** to those who need them." The Department's concern is not merely with producer compensation — it is with the downstream consumer impact of products being pulled from the market when carriers manipulate producer incentives.

### Producer Rights and Options

If you are appointed by a carrier that eliminates commissions mid-year or restricts access to products without advance notice, you have grounds to report this conduct to OID. The bulletin signals that OID is actively monitoring this behavior and will pursue enforcement action under § 1209(A) where warranted.

Producers should document any communications from carriers regarding mid-year commission changes, maintain records of their prior appointment terms, and consult with their professional association or legal counsel if they believe they have experienced this conduct.

## 1.4 Artificial Intelligence in Insurance: What Producers Need to Know

### The Regulatory Development

**OID Bulletin No. 2024-11**, issued November 14, 2024, requires all **authorized insurers** operating in Oklahoma to develop and maintain a written **AI Systems (AIS) Program**. This bulletin is directed at insurance companies, not individual producers — but it has important implications for producers who use AI tools or who sell products from carriers using AI in underwriting, rating, or claims.

### What the Carrier Obligation Covers

The AIS Program required of carriers must address:

**Governance:** Senior management accountability, policies across the AI lifecycle, documentation, oversight committees, and model management procedures.

**Risk Management:** Data quality and bias analysis, model validation and performance testing, consumer privacy protection, and detection of model drift (when an AI model's real-world performance diverges from its intended behavior).

**Third-Party Management:** Due diligence on AI vendors and data sources, contractual audit rights, and regulatory cooperation clauses.

**Compliance Standards:** AI-supported decisions must comply with the Unfair Trade Practices Act (36 O.S. § 1204), the Unfair Claims Settlement Practices Act (36 O.S. § 1250.5), and Property and Casualty Rating Laws.

The bulletin aligns with NAIC's 2020 AI Principles: fairness, accountability, transparency, and system security.

### Why This Matters to Producers

The core principle embedded in Bulletin 2024-11 is that **carriers — not their AI vendors — are responsible for the outcomes of AI-driven decisions**. If a carrier's AI system produces a discriminatory underwriting decision, denies a claim based on a biased model, or generates a rating that violates Oklahoma law, the carrier is liable. The vendor who built the AI is not.

This matters to producers for several reasons:

**You may be the first to notice.** Producers are often closer to clients and their specific circumstances than carrier underwriting departments. If a client is denied coverage or receives an adverse rating that seems inconsistent with their actual risk profile, this may be a sign of an AI model that is not performing as intended.

**Discriminatory AI outcomes are still illegal.** Under the Unfair Trade Practices Act, unfair discrimination in underwriting or rating is prohibited regardless of whether a human or an algorithm made the decision. A carrier cannot use AI as a shield against liability for discriminatory outcomes.

**Producers can report.** If you believe a carrier's AI-driven decision has adversely and unfairly affected a consumer, you can report this to OID. The Department has authority to request AI program documentation, model inventories, and validation records during examinations.

**Your own use of AI tools.** If you use AI tools in your own practice — for lead generation, client communication, document drafting, or sales support — the same principles apply. You remain responsible for the accuracy and fairness of advice you give to clients, regardless of whether an AI tool assisted in generating it.

## Chapter 1 Key Takeaways

1. **Annuity Best Interest Standard:** Oklahoma adopted NAIC Model #275 effective September 1, 2023. Producers must now act in the consumer's best interest — not merely recommend suitable products. Four obligations apply: Care, Disclosure, Conflict of Interest, and Documentation. Training requirements apply to new and existing annuity sellers.

2. **Anti-Rebating:** OID is actively investigating and finding commission kickback schemes. Returning any portion of commission as an inducement to write or keep business violates 36 O.S. § 1204(8)(a), regardless of how the arrangement is structured. Anti-rebating protects consumers by ensuring uniform pricing.

3. **Carrier Commission Practices:** Carriers eliminating commissions mid-year without notice may violate 36 O.S. § 1209(A). Producers have grounds to report this conduct to OID. OID has signaled it will pursue enforcement action.

4. **Artificial Intelligence:** Carriers must maintain a written AI Systems Program under Bulletin 2024-11. Carriers — not AI vendors — are responsible for AI-driven decisions. Producers play a role in identifying and reporting potentially discriminatory AI outcomes to OID.
